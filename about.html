about

Hi, I'm Gulnoza.

My first classroom wasn't a classroom at all — it was a nursery room for four-year-olds, and I was twenty-something, trying to figure out how to teach a child to count before I'd really figured out how to teach anything. That was 2018. I've been teaching, in one form or another, every year since.

I moved from that nursery room to a public school, teaching English to students from first grade through eighth, often four days a week, in classes of up to forty children. I learned quickly that no lesson plan survives contact with thirty-five eight-year-olds unchanged — so I started reaching for storytelling, songs, art, and outdoor games instead of just textbooks, because those were the things that actually got through.

"No lesson plan survives contact with forty children unchanged."

From the classroom to the lecture hall

In between, I trained as a translator, working between Uzbek, Russian, and English on legally binding documents — work that demands a kind of precision teaching doesn't always ask of you, and that taught me to never assume a word means the same thing in two languages. I also spent two years at Westminster International School as a teaching assistant, covering Science, English, and Mathematics across IEYC, IPC, IGCSE, and A-Level programs whenever a teacher was on leave, which meant learning to walk into someone else's classroom and keep it running without missing a beat.

In 2021, I won the British Council's IELTS WIN Prize — a small recognition, but one that pushed me to take my own English more seriously, the same way I was asking my students to take theirs seriously.

Eventually, I went back to being a student myself: an MA in Education and Innovation at Webster University Tashkent, finished with a 3.88 GPA. That degree didn't just add a credential — it gave me language for things I'd been doing instinctively for years: curriculum design, transformative learning, design thinking. I started seeing my own teaching differently once I had the theory to go with the instinct.

What I do now

Today I split my time between two roles at Webster University: as an Academic Affairs Officer, where I help recruit and onboard faculty and keep university data running smoothly, and as a faculty member teaching education and internship courses to students who are, themselves, becoming teachers. There's something quietly satisfying about training the next generation of educators, having spent years learning the same lessons myself, the hard way, in front of real classrooms.

"I'm teaching the people who will go on to teach — having learned it myself the hard way."

Outside of work, I run a small book exchange project, because the same thing that drew me to teaching — watching someone encounter an idea for the first time — is what draws me to books. This site is where those two things finally get to share a page.

Why this notebook exists

This site has three shelves: Books I'm reading and what they have to do with teaching (even when they don't, at first, seem to), Classroom notes from actual lessons, and Journey — the academic and career path that got me here, written honestly, including the parts I got wrong.

If you want new entries by email, you can subscribe from the homepage. No spam — just a new post when I have something worth saying.
